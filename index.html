When I joined as Head of Product Design, Truecaller had achieved well established growth with 400+ million users worldwide over 10 years, but the design organisation hadn't evolved to match this maturity.

The team consisted of talented individual contributors working across iOS, Android, and web platforms, but lacked the systematic foundations needed for design at scale.

The executive team had clear expectations for design transformation to support Truecaller's next phase of growth, primarily focussed at increasing market share in the US.

I led a comprehensive transformation of design operations, team capabilities and overall organisational design maturity through systematic assessment, strategic initiatives and collaborative leadership.

CONTEXT & LEADERSHIP ASKS

Exhaustive initial discussions with product and executive leaders at Truecaller revealed the following areas as immediate areas of work. These priorities shaped my strategic approach and provided clear success metrics for measuring leadership impact.

Operational Excellence: "We need design processes that can scale with our ambitions without sacrificing quality. Our current approach works for individual projects but breaks down at enterprise scale, especially at the speed we operate."

Cross-Platform Consistency: "Users expect seamless experiences across iOS and Android, but our platforms feel like different products. We need unified design that cements our brand identity while respecting platform conventions."

Strategic Design Partnership: "Design should be a strategic partner in product decisions, not just making things look good after we've decided what to build."

Team Development: "We have talented designers, but they need to level up their influence and collaboration with engineering and product teams. They also do not have a well-defined growth path yet."

ORGANISATIONAL CHALLENGES

Next, I moved onto have detailed discussions with all the designers, platform engineers and a few product owners at Truecaller, with the aim to identify and assess various process/people issues that were stopping them from doing their best work.

Through a series of structured 90-minute discussions with all designers and weekly collective fig-jam sessions, we started to openly talk about and uncover various wins and challenges that designers, PMs and engineers were facing while trying to ship their best work. We went through an analysis of current workflows, tool usage and collaboration patterns. Cross-functional dynamics and relationships with PMs came forth as a key area of improvement from all designers and product owners alike. The following themes started to emerge and repeat through all our conversations.

Process & Operations: Inconsistent design workflows, platform-specific silos, and limited cross-functional collaboration frameworks.

Team Capabilities: No clear growth path for designers, skill gaps in data analysis, increasing influence across product teams, and systematic design thinking.

Strategic Alignment: Design decisions made in isolation from business strategy, unclear design principles, reactive rather than proactive design planning.

Next, we also did a quick self assessment of skills with designers to identify Individual strengths, development areas, and career aspirations. These designers were creating high-quality UI work but operating in platform silos and decision vacuums leading to stunted vision in terms of how they see themselves grow in skillsets and capabilities.

The team had strong visual design skills but limited experience with quantitative analysis, resulting in design decisions based primarily on intuition rather than data integration. And more importantly, no shared understanding of design principles existed, leading to inconsistent user experiences and inefficient design reviews which were led by product leaders who at times, didn't necessarily feel well-equipped to direct designs for better.

My leadership philosophy was now well informed and centered around building sustainable systems that better equipped these designers to do their best work.
Rather than directing individual projects, I focused on creating the foundations, processes, and capabilities that would multiply the team's impact.

INITIATIVE 1: CROSS-PLATFORM DESIGN SYSTEM

Challenge: Platform fragmentation was creating inconsistent user experiences and inefficient design workflows.

Approach: Rather than imposing a top-down solution, I facilitated a collaborative design system creation process that respected platform conventions while establishing shared foundations. We also agreed that every designer would help me in their own way in identifying and adding to the component library.

Implementation Strategy

Discovery & Audit (4 weeks): Led comprehensive UI audit across iOS, Android, and web, identifying 150+ component variations and documenting platform-specific considerations.

Collaborative Design (8 weeks): Organised weekly design system workshops bringing together designers, iOS engineers, Android engineers to align on component specifications and interaction patterns.

Systematic Documentation (8 weeks): Created comprehensive design system documentation covering design tokens, component specifications, usage guidelines, and platform-specific implementations.

Adoption Framework (ongoing): Established minor governance checks during weekly design review meetings for new components to be handled, how to effectively contribute to component library and ease-of-handoff measures.

Results Achieved

This initiative once published, led to a significant reduction in design inconsistencies across platforms. We also observed ~40% faster design-to-development handoff process, especially for newly designed user flows. Improved design<>engineering collaboration with shared vocabulary and specifications, this led to ~90% adoption rate with both iOS and android engineering teams within 8months.

INITIATIVE 2: ESTABLISHING UX WRITING GUIDELINES

Challenge: Inconsistent terminology usage and content strategy was undermining user experience quality and creating localisation challenges.

Approach: Established comprehensive UX writing guidelines that addressed both immediate consistency needs and long-term content strategy.

Implementation Strategy

Content Audit & Analysis (3 weeks): Catalogued existing interface content across all platforms, identifying tone inconsistencies, clarity issues, and localisation gaps in major markets like India, Middle East and Latin America.

Guideline Development (5 weeks): Created comprehensive style guide covering voice and tone, grammar standards, platform-specific conventions, company specific abbreviations and accessibility requirements.

Documentation & Governance (2 weeks): Established review processes and approval workflows to maintain content consistency in future product development.

Training & Evangelism (ongoing): Delivered workshops for designers, product managers, and engineers on content strategy integration and implementation.

INITIATIVE 3: UP-SKILLING DESIGNERS FOR DATA-BACKED DECISION MAKING

Challenge: Team had strong design craft but needed enhanced skills in data analysis and evidence-based decision making.

Approach: Created structured weekly data workshops pairing designers with data analysts to build systematic data interpretation capabilities.

Weekly Data Workshop Program

Analyst Partnership Structure (ongoing): Paired each designer with a corresponding data analyst from their product vertical, creating dedicated learning partnerships and ongoing collaboration relationships.

Dashboard Mastery Sessions (8 weeks): Systematic walkthrough of existing company dashboards, teaching designers how to navigate analytics tools, interpret user behaviour data, and identify meaningful patterns in their respective product areas.

Usage Data Analysis Training (ongoing): Hands-on sessions focused on understanding user flow data, conversion metrics, feature adoption rates, and behavioural patterns specific to each designer's product domain.

Team Transformation Results

Enhanced decision-making confidence as designers began backing design choices with quantitative evidence.

Increased engagement with A/B testing with designers actively observing and learning from experiments in their teams.

Data-informed design culture emerging across the team as evidence-based thinking became standard practice now.

Increasing strategic influence at individual level as they could articulate design rationale using business metrics and user behaviour insights.

Challenge: Design decisions lacked strategic foundation and clear qualitative evaluation criteria.

Approach: Led and facilitated collaborative group discussions with design, engineering, and product leaders to establish core product principles that would guide both product strategy and design decision-making.

Critical Leadership Lessons

1. Coaching for Data-Driven Impact

Investing time in personally coaching designers through data literacy fundamentally transformed their influence and decision-making confidence. By pairing each designer with data analysts and walking through real dashboards together, I watched team members evolve from intuition-based to evidence-backed design thinking.

The weekly data workshops weren't just skill-building—they were confidence-building. Designers who previously felt uncertain in cross-functional discussions began advocating for their design decisions with quantitative backing, dramatically increasing their impact and strategic influence within their product teams.

2. Strategic Quick Wins Through Independence

During early days, identifying initiatives with minimal cross-functional dependencies proved crucial for building momentum and demonstrating design leadership value. The UX writing guidelines exemplified this approach—by focusing on copywriting consistency standards that the design team could implement independently, we achieved immediate visible improvements without lengthy approval cycles or complex stakeholder alignment.

These "low-hanging fruit" initiatives created early wins that built credibility and trust, making it easier to tackle more complex collaborative projects like design systems that required extensive cross-functional coordination.

3. Patience as a Strategic Leadership Tool

Evangelising design systems and principles across teams with different business objectives required sustained patience and persistence rather than force or urgency. Each team has legitimate competing priorities and timelines, so successful adoption means understanding their specific challenges and adapting the rollout accordingly.

Rather than mandating immediate compliance, I focussed on demonstrating value through pilot implementations with android payments team and celebrating early adopters. This patient approach is gradually building genuine buy-in, though full organisation-wide adoption remains an ongoing effort.
